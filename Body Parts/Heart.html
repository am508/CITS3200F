Cardiovascular disease is when you have damage to your heart and blood vessels. It includes heart attacks, heart failure, strokes and circulation problems. The major blood vessels are called arteries and veins. The arteries carry blood away from your heart which veins carry blood towards your heart. When cholesterol and other materials attach themselves to the blood vessels they reduce the room for blood to flow. High glucose levels can also reduce the room for blood to flow. Damage to your blood vessels can also cause clots to form on the walls of the blood vessels. If the clots break off, they are get stuck in narrow blood vessels and cause a blockage. A blockage of an artery can cause the part of the body it supplies to be deprived of oxygen and nutrients. Blood vessels can be damaged by high blood glucose, high blood pressure, smoking or high levels of cholesterol.

A heart attack can happen when the blood vessels to your heart are blocked, while a stroke can happen when the blood vessels to your brain are blocked. After a heart attack, your heart might not be able to pump as much blood which is known as heart failure. People with diabetes have a higher risk of heart attack, heart failure and stroke.

Heart attack, heart failure and stroke each have different warning signs, which can be different from person to person, but usually include:

  • Heart Attack
    • Chest pain or discomfort
    • Pain or discomfort in your arms, back, jaw, neck or stomach
    • Indigestion or nausea
    • Light-headedness
    • Shortness of breath
  • Heart Failure
    • Shortness of breath
    • Weakness
    • Nausea
    • Fatigue
    • Swelling of the feet and ankles because of fluid retention
  • Stroke
    • Weakness or numbness on one side of the body
    • Sudden confusion or trouble understanding
    • Trouble talking
    • Severe Headache
    • Trouble seeing out of one or both eyes
If you have the warning signs for heart attack, heart failure or stroke call 000 immediately.

There are many steps you can take to prevent a heart attack or stroke. Eating a healthy diet, being physically active and quitting smoking are important to help lower the risk of cardiovascular disease. It is also important to take any medication your doctor prescribes and to have your blood pressure checked at least twice a year and have your cholesterol checked at least once a year.
